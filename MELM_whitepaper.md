# A Morpheme-Event Language Model
## Toward Reality-Encoding Conversational AI at the Capability of a Five-Year-Old

**Whitepaper v1.0 — May 2026**
**Audience:** Strategic partners and ML researchers
**Author:** Independent research (5-year programme)

---

## Abstract

Frontier language models continue to absorb compute and parameters at a rate that has decoupled from the actual information content of language. We argue this is a tokenization, representation, and memory problem — not a scale problem. Drawing on five years of internal research and 2024–2026 advances in morpheme-aware tokenization (MorphBPE, MorphPiece, MoVoC-Tok), human-inspired episodic memory for LLMs (EM-LLM), state-space sequence models (Mamba-3), and developmentally-plausible pretraining (BabyLM 2024–2025), this whitepaper specifies the **Morpheme-Event Language Model (MELM)**: a four-layer architecture whose primitives are (i) linguistically-grounded morphemes and (ii) discrete *events* that compose into episodic memory. The MVP target is a sub-1B-parameter, CPU-deployable model that demonstrates the conversational competence of a typical five-year-old (≈ 5,000–10,000 word receptive vocabulary, ~12,000-morpheme inventory, multi-turn dialogue, simple counterfactual reasoning). We also describe a reproducible protocol for bootstrapping the training corpus from a *strategic combination* of Simple English Wikipedia, CHILDES, OpenAssistant, and synthetic data generated by a teacher LLM (DeepSeek V4 or a comparable local model). The paper closes with the four open research questions that gate scientific success.

## 1. Executive Summary

The field has assumed that better language models require more parameters, more tokens, and more compute. The Phi-4 result (14B parameters, mostly synthetic textbook data, beating Llama-3.1-405B on STEM reasoning) is the loudest recent counterexample. The deeper observation: language has *small generative grammar* and *large redundant surface*. A model that operates on the grammar — morphemes for words, events for memory — should need dramatically less of everything.

**The three claims this paper defends:**

1. **A ~12,000-morpheme inventory plus ~200 productive affixes is sufficient for five-year-old conversational language.** This is a tight, falsifiable target backed by morpholexical statistics (MorphoLex covers 70,000+ English words from a derivational base of similar size) and child language acquisition data.
2. **Memory is not a context window; it is a compressed graph of events.** Following EM-LLM (Fountas et al., 2024) and SEEM (2026), we treat memory as a temporally-ordered set of episodic events with structured retrieval. This is more parameter-efficient than dense RAG indices and more faithful to how children actually accumulate experience.
3. **The training corpus is the bottleneck — and it can be bootstrapped automatically.** Using a four-stage pipeline (curate → morphologically segment → semantically annotate → augment with teacher-generated dialogue), a developmentally-plausible 100M-word corpus can be produced in weeks, not years.

**The MVP**: a 300–800M parameter hybrid (Mamba-3 backbone + structured episodic memory module) trained on ~100M morpheme-segmented words plus 50M tokens of synthetic child-grounded dialogue, validated against the BabyLM evaluation suite extended with episodic recall tasks.

## 2. The Thesis: Reality Encoding via Compression

### 2.1 What language actually compresses

A useful frame: language is a *lossy compression scheme for events in time*. A sentence is a serialised description of an action or state-change. The morphology of that sentence carries the slot structure (agent, action, patient, manner, time) and the lexicon fills in the slots. Children acquire this scheme efficiently because their brains have strong inductive biases toward (a) finding minimal meaning-bearing units (morphemes), (b) segmenting continuous experience into discrete events, and (c) composing the two.

LLMs trained on subword BPE tokens recover most of this *implicitly* and *expensively*. They burn parameters memorising statistical correlations that a morphologically-aware model could express directly. They have no architectural notion of "event" — context is a flat token stream that the attention mechanism must sift on every forward pass. World knowledge is entangled with surface form across all weight matrices.

### 2.2 Why the compression argument has limits

To pre-empt a fair criticism: **storage compression ≠ semantic compression.** Wikipedia compresses to ~10 GB through textual redundancy, but answering questions about Wikipedia requires representing decompressed semantic content. Morpheme-awareness shrinks the *input* representation; it does not shrink the *world-knowledge* you need to retain. That cost migrates from the tokenizer into the weight matrix or — in our design — into an explicit episodic store.

This reframes the engineering problem cleanly. We are not promising a 100× parameter reduction across the board. We are promising:

- A ~5–10× reduction in *input representation cost* through morpheme primitives;
- A *qualitative change* in long-context behaviour by replacing flat token attention with structured event retrieval;
- Sufficient world knowledge for a five-year-old's conversational scope, achievable in 300–800M parameters.

### 2.3 The five-year-old benchmark

Why a five-year-old? Three reasons. First, it is a well-characterised cognitive milestone: typically-developing children at age 5 have a receptive vocabulary in the 5,000–10,000 word range, fluent multi-turn dialogue, theory of mind, simple counterfactual reasoning, and the ability to narrate sequenced events. Second, it sits well below the capability ceiling that current LLMs must clear, so it is achievable with modest compute. Third, it is the developmental level at which *episodic memory becomes robust* — children under five rarely retain coherent autobiographical memory, so a system that demonstrates five-year-old conversation has by definition implemented working episodic memory.

This is not a small target. It is the smallest target with cognitive coherence.

## 3. Prior Art and Where MELM Sits

### 3.1 Morphologically-aware tokenization

The closest direct ancestors of MELM's input layer:

- **Morfessor** (Creutz & Lagus, 2007) — unsupervised morpheme segmentation; the foundational tool, still widely used as a baseline.
- **MorphPiece** (Jabbar, 2023) — combines a morph-table with BPE for English, demonstrating linguistic alignment.
- **MorphBPE** (2025) — constrains BPE merges so that they cannot cross gold morpheme boundaries; reported gains on morphologically rich languages.
- **MoVoC-Tok** and **TreeTok** (2025) — vocabulary construction from frequent morphemes plus data-driven subwords; unsupervised morphological tree induction.
- **Morphology-aware subword tokenization for Slovak** (2026) and the seven-language cross-lingual neural segmentation study (Olbrich & Žabokrtský, 2026) confirm that morpheme alignment is reliably learnable across diverse typologies.

Empirical pattern: the gain from morpheme-awareness is largest in low-resource and morphologically-rich settings, and modest in high-resource English. MELM weaponises this by treating the *training corpus itself* as a controlled-resource setting (~100M words), where the inductive bias should compound.

### 3.2 Episodic memory for LLMs

- **EM-LLM** (Fountas et al., 2024) — segments token streams into events using *Bayesian surprise* (negative log-likelihood of next token), refines event boundaries with graph-theoretic modularity, and retrieves with a two-stage similarity-plus-temporal-contiguity process. Outperforms full-context baselines on LongBench at 10M-token scales.
- **SEEM — Structured Episodic Event Memory** (2026) — dual-layer representation: a Graph Memory Layer for static facts, an Episodic Memory Layer for event-centric details.
- **Position: Episodic Memory is the Missing Piece for Long-Term LLM Agents** (Pink et al., 2025) and the "Memory in the Age of AI Agents" survey (2025) frame episodic memory as the principled alternative to ever-longer context windows and ever-larger RAG indices.

MELM adopts EM-LLM's surprise-based segmentation as its default event boundary detector and SEEM's dual-layer (semantic + episodic) split as the memory architecture.

### 3.3 Compositional architectures

Tree-LSTMs (Tai et al., 2015), Recursive Neural Networks (Socher et al., 2011–2013), Recurrent Neural Network Grammars (Dyer et al., 2016), and Tree Stack Memory Units (2019) all explored explicit compositional structure. They produced strong per-parameter results on compositional generalization but did not scale, because the inductive bias was too rigid for general-domain text. MELM applies compositional bias *only at the morpheme-to-word boundary*, where the compositional rules are tight, and reverts to standard sequence modelling above the word level.

### 3.4 Efficient sub-1B architectures (2026)

- **Mamba-3** (March 2026) — improved selective state-space model; sub-quadratic compute and constant memory inference. The 1.5B-scale model improves average downstream accuracy by 1.8 points over strong baselines.
- **RWKV v5 "Eagle"** — parallel-trainable, RNN-at-inference; deployed on 1.5 billion Windows machines for on-device Copilot. Demonstrates that constant-memory CPU inference at 1B-scale is a shipped reality.
- **Phi-3 / Phi-4** — confirms that *data quality and structure* beat raw scale at small parameter counts. Phi-4 is mostly synthetic.
- **Hybrid SSM+attention** stacks (Jamba family, Hymba) — combine the strengths cleanly.

MELM's backbone is a Mamba-3 hybrid: linear-time sequence modelling for the bulk of the network, with a *single structured-attention layer* dedicated to event composition.

### 3.5 Developmentally-plausible pretraining

The **BabyLM Challenge** (2023, 2024, planned 2025) constrains pretraining to ≤100M words from sources approximating child input: CHILDES (child-directed speech, birth–13 yrs), Project Gutenberg children's books, BNC dialogue, OpenSubtitles, Simple English Wikipedia. The 2024 findings paper documents that *corpus selection and filtering* explain more variance in downstream performance than architecture choice at this scale. **BabyBabelLM** (2025) extends this multilingually. MELM's training data plan is a direct extension of the BabyLM rules.

### 3.6 Lessons from speech systems (STT/TTS)

A reviewer asked an unusually productive question: do speech-side systems already exploit morpheme-aware and event-aware primitives, and what can we learn? The answer is *yes, in pieces, and the convergent evidence strengthens MELM's case considerably*.

**ASR has independently rediscovered morpheme-aware tokenization for low-resource and morphologically-rich languages.** Recent work for Malayalam, Slovak, Amharic, Yoloxóchitl Mixtec, and a broad survey of African languages (ACL AfricanNLP 2025) all converge on the same conclusion: subword vocabularies aligned to morphemes (S-BPE, MorphBPE, syllable-BPE) reduce word error rate substantially in agglutinative and templatic languages where word-level vocabularies blow up combinatorially. This is *direct empirical validation of the MELM thesis from an adjacent modality*. ASR teams arrived at this conclusion not from theory but from forced encounter with morphological productivity.

**G2P (grapheme-to-phoneme) is the speech-side analog of morphological analysis.** Modern TTS pipelines (NaturalSpeech 1/2, VALL-E, Piper, NVIDIA NeMo) keep an explicit phoneme front-end despite end-to-end pressure to remove it. Phoneme pre-training is a *core module* in NaturalSpeech because language-level abstraction over orthographic surface form is too informationally valuable to discard. Recent G2P work (OLaPh, 2025; Rethinking G2P with Rich Data, 2025; LLM-aided G2P, 2024) shows the same pattern: rich, structured front-ends outperform purely data-driven approaches on morphologically rich languages like German and Persian. **MELM's morphological front-end is the same architectural decision applied to text.** The fact that TTS engineers fought to keep G2P even as everything else became neural is strong evidence for an analogous decision in language modelling.

**Self-supervised speech models discover articulatory primitives.** Wav2Vec 2.0, HuBERT, and the SPARC / ARTI-6 frameworks (2024–2026) demonstrate that latent representations of speech organise themselves around *articulatory features* (place and manner of articulation, voicing) — discrete, compositional primitives drawn from a small inventory. The 6-dimensional articulatory encoding of ARTI-6 is, philosophically, the same compression argument MELM makes for text: the underlying generative grammar has a small primitive set, and a model that respects it generalises with less data. SPARC explicitly produces accent-preserving zero-shot voice conversion because the disentangled primitive encoding *factors out* speaker identity from articulation.

**Prosody is the speech-side event boundary.** Prosodic boundary detection — pitch reset, pause, declination — has been a robust acoustic correlate of *intonation units*, which themselves correspond closely to event boundaries in narrative discourse. A 2025 paper modifies Whisper to flag intonation unit boundaries directly (Disentanglement of Prosodic Meaning, PNAS 2025) and matches or exceeds human annotators with modest training data. The CNN-based prosodic boundary detectors of recent years achieve ~90% on phrase boundary detection. **This is an independent multimodal signal for the same event-segmentation problem EM-LLM solves with Bayesian surprise on text.** A multimodal MELM could fuse the two signals, with prosodic boundaries providing strong supervision in audio-grounded training.

**Whisper itself sits in the awkward middle.** Whisper uses GPT-2 byte-level BPE — designed for English — refit but not redesigned for multilingual ASR. The 2025 paper "Beyond WER: Probing Whisper's Sub-token Decoder Across Diverse Language Resource Levels" documents the predictable consequence: substantial performance gaps on languages typologically distant from Indo-European training data. This is the *negative example* for MELM's thesis: a frontier-quality acoustic model, hobbled in its outputs by a tokenizer that was never designed for the linguistic diversity it encounters. The same critique applies, *a fortiori*, to text-only LLMs.

**Implications for MELM design.** Three concrete updates emerge:

1. **Phonological grounding is a viable secondary representation.** A future variant of MELM (call it MELM-Phon) could ground morpheme embeddings in phonological feature vectors (IPA-style) rather than purely orthographic tokens. This would unlock cross-script transfer (English ↔ Russian ↔ Arabic without script mapping) and natural integration with speech.
2. **Prosodic boundaries should be incorporated into multimodal training.** When MELM is extended to audio-grounded data (a likely Phase 2), prosodic boundary detection should serve as an auxiliary supervision signal for the Event Processor's segmentation, complementing the Bayesian-surprise heuristic.
3. **The articulatory-feature paradigm validates the compositional embedding approach.** Layer 2's Tree-LSTM composition is doing for morphemes what SPARC does for phonemes: extracting a small, compositional primitive set from a complex surface signal. The fact that the speech-side approach produces high-quality intelligible output from 6 articulatory dimensions is the strongest evidence that aggressive compression on the right primitives preserves expressivity.

### 3.7 Where MELM is novel

No existing system combines: morphological front-end + event-segmented episodic store + sub-1B Mamba backbone + developmentally-plausible corpus + compositional embedding. Each piece is published; the synthesis is not. The closest single proposal is the Princeton compmem paper "Towards LLMs with human-like episodic memory" (Dong et al., 2025), which articulates the cognitive case but does not specify an end-to-end architecture or a corpus-construction protocol.

## 4. Architecture: The Morpheme-Event Language Model (MELM)

MELM is a four-layer stack. Each layer is independently testable.

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Layer 4 — Morphological Generation (output decoder)        │
│   Morpheme sequence → surface form via FST + neural        │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                           ▲
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Layer 3 — Event Processor + Reasoning Backbone             │
│   Mamba-3 hybrid (300–800M params) + Episodic Memory       │
│   (semantic graph layer + event-indexed episodic layer)    │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                           ▲
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Layer 2 — Compositional Embedding                          │
│   Morpheme vectors composed via tight tree composition     │
│   {root, prefix, suffix, inflection} → word embedding      │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                           ▲
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Layer 1 — Morphological Front-End                          │
│   FST analyzer (CELEX/MorphoLex/Morfessor) + neural fallb. │
│   Surface form → morpheme tuple with semantic tags         │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 4.1 Layer 1 — Morphological front-end

A finite-state transducer compiled from MorphoLex (70,000+ English words) and CELEX, with Morfessor as an unsupervised fallback for OOV cases. Output is a tuple `(root, prefix*, suffix*, inflection*)` plus part-of-speech and a small set of semantic tags (negation, agentivity, modality, tense). Coverage target: 99% of word forms in CHILDES + 95% of word forms in Simple English Wikipedia.

This replaces the standard BPE tokenizer. Vocabulary size: **target 12,000 morphemes** (see open question Q1). For comparison, GPT-2's BPE vocabulary is 50,257; Llama 3's is 128,000.

### 4.2 Layer 2 — Compositional embedding

Each morpheme is a dense vector (~256-dimensional). Word-level embeddings are computed by a tight composition function over the morpheme tuple — concretely, a single Tree-LSTM cell with depth ≤3, sufficient for nearly all English derivational morphology. The composition is differentiable end-to-end, and pre-trained on a morpheme-tagged subset of the BabyLM corpus.

This produces dense word embeddings that *factor* — `unbreakable` is mechanically `[NEG] ⊕ [break] ⊕ [POSSIBLE]`. Out-of-distribution morpheme combinations decode correctly because the composition function generalises by construction.

### 4.3 Layer 3 — Event Processor (EP) and reasoning backbone

The reasoning backbone is a Mamba-3 hybrid: 24 SSM layers + 4 attention layers. Total parameters: 300M (small) / 800M (target MVP). Why hybrid: pure SSMs are weak on associative recall; targeted attention layers fix this without paying the full quadratic cost.

Sitting in parallel with the backbone is the **Event Processor**. The EP maintains two stores:

- **Semantic Graph Layer (SGL):** an entity–relation graph populated with stable facts (the cat lives in the kitchen; the cat is named Mochi). Entries are inserted when an event has high semantic but low temporal salience. Storage: dense vectors keyed by entity hash, ~10MB at MVP scale.
- **Episodic Memory Layer (EML):** a temporally-ordered list of *event tokens*. Each event token is a compressed vector summary of a contiguous span of input, with metadata `(t_start, t_end, surprise, salient_entities)`. Boundaries between events are detected with EM-LLM's Bayesian-surprise method. Storage: ~1KB per event; for a five-year-old's lifetime conversational scope (~10,000 events) this is ~10MB.

**Structured attention mask.** When the backbone needs to retrieve, it issues a query into both stores. Retrieval is two-stage: similarity-based candidate selection in the EML, then temporal-contiguity expansion (returning neighbouring events in time, mirroring how human episodic recall works). The attention mask between the backbone and the retrieved events is *structured* — identity for current context, sparse and event-keyed for retrieved memory. Whether this mask is *designed* or *learned* is open question Q2.

### 4.4 Layer 4 — Morphological generation (output decoder)

Generation is the inverse of Layer 1. The model predicts a sequence of morpheme tuples, which are mapped to surface forms by a generation FST. This handles inflection robustly: the model never has to memorise that `go → went`; it predicts `(go, PAST)` and the FST realises it. Irregular forms are stored as overrides in the FST, learned from the lexicon.

### 4.5 Parameter budget

| Component | Parameters | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Morpheme embeddings (12k × 256) | 3M | Trainable |
| Layer 2 composition (Tree-LSTM) | 2M | Lightweight |
| Layer 3 backbone (Mamba-3 hybrid) | 250–700M | Bulk of model |
| Event Processor (encoder + projector) | 30M | Dedicated to event summarization |
| Output projection | 3M | Tied to morpheme embeddings where possible |
| **Total (MVP target)** | **≈ 800M** | CPU-runnable with int8 quantization |

By comparison, Phi-3-mini is 3.8B and runs on a phone. MELM-MVP at ~800M with constant-memory inference is conservatively within the same deployment envelope.

## 5. Training Corpus Strategy

This is the strategic heart of the project. The corpus is the bottleneck, and prior work (Phi-3/4, BabyLM 2024 findings) confirms that *data quality and structure* are the dominant lever at this scale.

### 5.1 Source mix

The MVP corpus is constructed from four pillars, each chosen for a specific quality:

| Source | Word count | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **CHILDES (English subset)** | ~5M | Developmental grounding; child-directed speech as syntactic core |
| **Simple English Wikipedia** | ~30M | Encyclopaedic world knowledge in age-appropriate register |
| **OpenAssistant Conversations (oasst1, English)** | ~20M | Multi-turn dialogue templates; instruction-following structure |
| **Children's books (Gutenberg + curated)** | ~15M | Narrative coherence, event sequences |
| **Synthetic dialogue (teacher-LLM generated)** | ~30M | Targeted gap-filling: counterfactual reasoning, theory-of-mind dialogues, event recombination tasks |
| **Total** | ~100M | Within BabyLM Challenge budget |

This is deliberately at or below the BabyLM 100M-word ceiling so that public results are directly comparable.

### 5.2 The four-stage construction pipeline

**Stage 1 — Curate.** Pull from each source. Deduplicate aggressively. Apply a child-suitability classifier (toxic content, NSFW, advanced ideology) trained on a small labelled set. Drop documents that fail.

**Stage 2 — Morphologically segment.** Run every word through the Layer-1 FST. Words that fail the FST go to Morfessor; persistent failures are flagged for manual review. Output: a parallel corpus of `(surface_form, morpheme_tuple)` pairs. This is the single most expensive offline step but is one-time.

**Stage 3 — Semantically annotate (event-level).** For each sentence, run an AMR (Abstract Meaning Representation) parser. Group AMRs into *event units* by clustering on overlapping participants and temporal connectives. The output is, per document, a list of event records `(event_id, predicate, args, participants, time_index)`. We use AMR rather than UCCA because the parser ecosystem is more mature; the open question Q3 considers a UCCA bridge.

**Stage 4 — Augment with synthetic dialogue.** A teacher LLM (DeepSeek V4 via API, or a local 70B equivalent such as Llama 3.3 70B) generates targeted training data for capability gaps the four organic sources do not cover well:

- Counterfactual reasoning ("If the cat had not jumped, the cup would not have…")
- Theory of mind ("Mochi thinks the food is in the bowl, but you moved it.")
- Event recombination across episodic boundaries ("Yesterday you told me about… today, how does that connect to…")
- Self-correction and clarification dialogues

Synthetic data is templated, not free-generation: the teacher LLM is given a pre-existing event record from Stage 3 and asked to produce a child-appropriate dialogue *grounded* in that event. This grounding step is critical — it is the difference between Phi-style "high-quality textbook" data and the slop of unconditioned synthetic generation.

The DeepSeek-R1 distillation pipeline (which produced 800k high-quality synthetic reasoning samples in the original release) is an explicit template here. Their methodology is reproducible with any sufficiently capable teacher model.

### 5.3 Quality controls

- **Vocabulary cap.** Reject any sentence that requires more than 12,000 distinct morphemes to express.
- **Reading-age filter.** Run each generated text through a Flesch-Kincaid filter; target reading age ≤ 8.
- **Event-density floor.** Reject documents with < 1 event per 80 tokens (low information density).
- **Held-out probe set.** Reserve 10% of CHILDES specifically for evaluation, never for training.

### 5.4 Estimated effort

Pipeline implementation: 3–4 engineer-months. Compute for Stage 2–4 on the full corpus: < $5,000 at 2026 prices. The teacher-LLM API spend (Stage 4) is the largest variable line item; ~30M tokens of grounded synthetic dialogue at DeepSeek-V4 prices is ~$300–$600.

## 6. Evaluation Plan

A model that claims five-year-old conversational competence must clear a structured evaluation. We propose three benchmark families:

### 6.1 BabyLM-aligned evaluation

Run the standard BabyLM 2024/2025 evaluation suite (BLiMP for syntactic generalization, GLUE-derived NLU subset, EWoK for world knowledge). Pass criterion: outperform the BabyLM 2024 strict-track baseline at equivalent parameter count.

### 6.2 Five-year-old conversational benchmark (new)

A bespoke suite, designed in collaboration with developmental psycholinguists, covering:

- **Receptive vocabulary** — Peabody-style picture-vocabulary probes.
- **Productive grammar** — elicited production of past tense, plural, comparative, possessive, with a 50/50 mix of regular and irregular targets.
- **Multi-turn dialogue coherence** — three-turn dialogues scored for reference resolution, on-topic continuation, and politeness.
- **Theory of mind** — adapted Sally-Anne and false-belief tasks at age-appropriate complexity.
- **Counterfactual reasoning** — short scenarios with "what if" prompts.

### 6.3 Episodic recall (the differentiator)

This is where MELM should pull away from BPE+RAG baselines:

- **Same-day recall** — within a session, retrieve event details after distractor turns.
- **Cross-day recall** — retrieve event details after persistence and reload.
- **Event recombination** — answer questions that require linking two events not previously linked.
- **Temporal contiguity recall** — given a target event, recall the immediately preceding and following events. This directly probes the temporal-contiguity retrieval mechanism inherited from EM-LLM.

### 6.4 Baselines

Three head-to-head baselines, all matched on training compute:

1. **BPE-baseline:** identical Mamba-3 backbone, identical corpus, standard BPE tokenizer, no episodic memory.
2. **RAG-baseline:** same backbone, BPE, with a vector-store RAG over the corpus.
3. **Full-context-baseline:** same backbone, BPE, with the maximum context window the architecture supports.

A successful MELM should outperform (1) on per-parameter generalization, outperform (2) on episodic recall and parameter cost, and approach (3) on quality at a fraction of the compute.

## 7. Open Research Questions

These four questions gate scientific success. They are explicitly the ones the founder named, plus two we believe emerge naturally from the design.

### Q1. What is the minimum morpheme vocabulary sufficient for five-year-old-level conversation? Is 12,000 the right number, or smaller?

The 12,000 figure is an upper bound informed by MorphoLex (which catalogues derivational morphology for 70,000+ English words) and the assumption that a five-year-old's productive vocabulary of ~5,000 words plus inflectional morphology can be expressed in roughly 1,500–3,000 lemmas × ~100 productive affixes. The honest answer is that this is empirically unknown.

**Test plan:** train MELM-MVP at 4 vocabulary sizes — 4k, 8k, 12k, 20k — holding everything else constant. Measure (a) BabyLM benchmarks, (b) coverage on a held-out CHILDES set, (c) the rate of OOV-fallback to Morfessor. We expect a hockey-stick: minimal gains beyond ~10–12k for English, but the curve will look very different in morphologically richer languages (open question Q4).

### Q2. Can the structured attention mask in the Event Processor be *learned* rather than designed, while still converging on linguistically correct structure?

EM-LLM's surprise-based segmentation is unsupervised but heuristic. The graph-theoretic refinement step uses fixed modularity scoring. A more elegant — and more honest — design would have the attention mask emerge from training pressure alone.

**Test plan:** ablation across three configurations — fixed mask (current EM-LLM), learnable-with-prior (initialise to surprise-based segmentation, allow gradient), learned-from-scratch. Measure final performance and the *correlation* of learned event boundaries with (a) human-annotated event boundaries on a small probe set, (b) AMR-derived gold event boundaries from Stage 3 of the pipeline. The interesting outcome is *qualitative*: do learned masks recover linguistically meaningful structure, or do they overfit to surrogate features?

### Q3. How does MELM compare to Retrieval-Augmented Generation on episodic recall? Is explicit event storage more efficient than dense retrieval?

The 2025 RAG-vs-Memory survey makes the conceptual case that they serve different roles: RAG is good for static enterprise knowledge; episodic memory is good for dynamic interaction history. But the actual head-to-head benchmark — *same backbone, same corpus, episodic vs RAG* — has not been published cleanly.

**Test plan:** Section 6.3 evaluation with the RAG-baseline and a dense Memory-augmented baseline using LongRAG-style chunking. Measure (a) recall accuracy on cross-day retrieval, (b) tokens-per-query (cost), (c) parameter overhead per stored event. We expect MELM to win decisively on event recombination and temporal contiguity, while RAG remains competitive on flat factual retrieval.

A clean comparison here is, on its own, a publishable result.

### Q4. Is there a clean mapping between MELM's event representation and formal semantic representations (AMR, UCCA) that would allow cross-system interoperability?

The Stage 3 annotation pipeline already uses AMR. The deeper question: are the *learned* event tokens of the EML interpretable as sub-graphs of AMR or UCCA? If yes, MELM's memory becomes inspectable, debuggable, and *exportable* to symbolic systems.

**Test plan:** train a probing classifier from event tokens to AMR sub-graph predictions. If accuracy is high, the mapping is recoverable. The 2025 AMR survey (Survey of Abstract Meaning Representation: Then, Now, Future) gives the relevant framework. Bonus: the DMR 2025 workshop community is already debating cross-formalism interop, providing a natural publishing venue.

### Q5 (additional). Can the morpheme embedding space be shared across languages, and does cross-lingual morphological proximity improve low-resource performance?

Recent work (Cross-Linguistic Transfer in Multilingual NLP: The Role of Language Families and Morphology, 2025) shows that morphological similarity is a *better* predictor of cross-lingual transfer success than lexical similarity. This is a strong prior in MELM's favour. BabyBabelLM (2025) provides developmentally-plausible corpora in multiple languages.

**Test plan:** train multilingual MELM with a shared morpheme embedding space (using a unified IPA-like phonological backbone) across English, Turkish, and Swahili — three typologically distinct, well-resourced-enough languages. Measure transfer to a held-out fourth language (e.g., Yoruba) at multiple data-budget points. If morphological proximity dominates, this is the key to the multilingual extension and a strong narrative for low-resource deployments.

### Q6 (additional). At what corpus scale does the morpheme-aware advantage saturate?

The morpheme-aware gain is largest in low-resource regimes. As corpus scale increases, BPE eventually catches up. Where exactly is the crossover? Knowing this defines the efficient frontier of MELM-style architectures.

**Test plan:** scan corpus size from 10M to 1B words. Measure performance gap (MELM vs BPE-baseline) at each point. Identify the saturation knee.

## 8. Roadmap & MVP Milestones — Compressed 6-Month Path to MVP

The original roadmap envisioned a ~14-month cadence. After auditing what is actually buildable with off-the-shelf 2026 components, **the time to a publicly-demonstrable MELM-MVP can be compressed to six months at a total budget of ~$400–500K.** This section documents how.

### 8.1 The three things that change the math

Three findings collapse the original timeline:

**(i) EM-LLM works as a zero-training wrapper on any transformer-based LLM.** The official EM-LLM repository (github.com/em-llm/EM-LLM-model) supports Mistral, Llama 3, Llama 3.1, Phi-3-mini, and Phi-3.5-mini base models out of the box, with no fine-tuning required. *This means the entire Layer 3 Event Processor can be validated in Week 1 against a frozen pretrained base, decoupled from morpheme work entirely.* The original plan implicitly built EM-LLM-equivalent functionality from scratch; instead, we drop it in.

**(ii) Pretrained Mamba checkpoints are public.** state-spaces/mamba-{130m, 370m, 790m, 1.4b, 2.8b}-hf are openly available on HuggingFace, plus NVIDIA's Nemotron 3 Super (March 2026), an open Mamba-Transformer hybrid. We warm-start the Layer 3 backbone from a 790M Mamba checkpoint rather than pretraining from scratch. The morpheme tokenizer (Layer 1) and compositional embedding (Layer 2) become the only new modules trained from random initialization; Layer 3's body is fine-tuned, not pretrained.

**(iii) BabyLM 2024 100M corpus is downloadable.** The corpus is published on HuggingFace and through the BabyLM GitHub. It is already deduplicated, child-suitability-filtered, and split into the canonical 100M-word strict track. *We do not construct a corpus.* We download it and run morpheme segmentation + AMR annotation on top. This eliminates the original M0 (4-month corpus pipeline) and replaces it with two weeks of preprocessing.

Combined, these three findings convert the longest-pole work-streams into rapid integrations of mature open-source components. The original whitepaper-style 14-month plan was honest about a *from-scratch* implementation; the field has matured enough in 2025–2026 that a *from-components* implementation is dramatically faster.

Everything else is integration of mature open-source.** This is the same playbook Phi-3 (Microsoft) and Mistral followed at larger scale — the engineering ratio that Phi achieved against larger models was driven by data and integration, not novel infrastructure.

### 8.4 Compute budget walk-through (this is the surprising part)

For a 790M-parameter model trained on 200M tokens (BabyLM 100M + 30M synthetic + 70M repeated for curriculum / multi-epoch):

- Training FLOPs ≈ 6 × 8e8 × 2e8 ≈ 1e18 FLOPs
- H100 BF16 throughput ≈ 700 TFLOPS = 7e14 FLOPs/sec
- Single H100 wall-clock ≈ 1,400 sec ≈ 23 min for one full pass
- 8× H100 cluster ≈ 3 min
- Realistic with overhead, gradient accumulation, eval breaks: ~30 min per training run
- Architecture ablations across the project: ~50 full runs ≈ 25 GPU-hours per ablation
- Total compute: 50 × 25 ≈ 1,250 H100-hours
- Cost at Lambda PCIe ($2.49/hr): **~$3,100 of compute for the entire MVP training programme**

Even with a 10× safety margin for re-runs, evaluation runs, and exploratory work, the total compute spend is **under $40K**. This is shockingly cheap compared to the prevailing assumption that foundational language model work requires seven-figure compute budgets. The reason is straightforward: at sub-1B scale on a 100M-word corpus, the Chinchilla-optimal training compute simply isn't very large. The Phi-3 mini paper notes the same — Phi-3-mini (3.8B) was trained for 3.3T tokens, but that was a deliberate over-training choice for capability, not a Chinchilla-optimum. MELM at 800M / 200M tokens is *under-Chinchilla* by design, traded against the morpheme inductive bias and the warm-start from a pretrained Mamba.

### 8.7 Per-month kill criteria (modest-funding discipline)

If any of the following fails, halt and re-plan rather than push through:

- **End of Month 1:** Layer 1 FST coverage on Simple English Wikipedia ≥ 95%; EM-LLM wrapper on Phi-3.5-mini matches or exceeds the published numbers on a single LongBench task. *Otherwise: morpheme thesis or EP integration broken — stop.*
- **End of Month 2:** MELM-Tiny achieves ≥ 80% of the BabyLM 2024 strict-track baseline at equivalent parameter count. *Otherwise: morpheme tokenizer + Mamba integration has a bug — debug before scaling.*
- **End of Month 3:** Synthetic data rejection rate < 30%; MELM-Small ≥ 90% of BabyLM baseline. *Otherwise: synthetic pipeline grounding is failing — fix before adding more.*
- **End of Month 4:** MELM-MVP outperforms MELM-Small by ≥ 5% on BabyLM. *Otherwise: scaling not paying — investigate before publishing.*
- **End of Month 5:** MELM-MVP equals or beats BPE-baseline on per-parameter generalization AND wins clearly on episodic recall. *This is the thesis pass-fail.*

### 8.8 What this compressed plan does NOT include (deliberate scope discipline)

To hit six months, the following are *explicitly deferred* to a post-MVP Phase 2:

- Multilingual extension (originally M4) — defer entirely; English-only MVP
- Phonological grounding / MELM-Phon variant — note in paper, defer build
- Audio modality / prosodic boundary fusion — defer
- Q4 (AMR/UCCA interoperability formal study) — collect data during MVP, publish separately
- Q6 (corpus-scale saturation study) — defer; needs >MVP compute

The MVP demonstrates the thesis at the smallest scope that is scientifically interesting. Everything else is a post-MVP follow-up that becomes vastly easier to fund once the demo exists.

### 8.9 Why we believe modest funding works here

The single most expensive line item in any AI startup is *training a frontier model* — sums measured in tens to hundreds of millions of dollars. MELM-MVP is *not* a frontier model. It is a 800M-parameter, 200M-token, warm-started, fine-tuned-not-pretrained, sub-1B model. The compute cost is rounding-error compared to typical AI startup costs.

The remaining cost is people. Three senior engineers for six months is well within the angel-check range targeted by the Tier-A investors in the accompanying outreach list (AI Grant cycles fund $250K-2M; Conviction writes $500K-2M seed checks; Pioneer grants are smaller but explicitly research-friendly). A $500K SAFE at $10M cap from a single Tier-A AI angel covers the entire MVP with comfortable buffer.

The bet is that a working demo at Month 6 — a 800M-parameter model running on a laptop, holding a coherent conversation with five-year-old register, demonstrating cross-day episodic recall that BPE+RAG baselines cannot match — re-prices the round. It is the cheapest credible path we know of from thesis to demo for a foundational AI architecture bet.

## 9. Risks and What Would Falsify the Thesis

We will know we were wrong if:

- **R1 — The morphological front-end fails to scale to general text.** If FST coverage on Simple English Wikipedia falls below 95%, the Morfessor fallback rate will dominate and the linguistic-bias advantage erodes.
- **R2 — Episodic memory does not beat RAG in a clean head-to-head.** If MELM cannot demonstrate clear wins on cross-day recall and event recombination at equal compute, the architectural premise weakens.
- **R3 — The compositional embedding does not generalise.** If novel morpheme combinations (e.g., productive new compounds) fail at a higher rate than BPE, the "compositional decode" promise is broken.
- **R4 — The five-year-old benchmark is unreachable at 800M parameters.** This is the largest single risk. If we cannot reach it at 800M, we will know the world-knowledge floor is higher than expected and rescope to 1.5–2B.

Each risk has an explicit kill criterion attached to a milestone. We do not advance to the next milestone without clearing the prior one.

## 10. Conclusion

The thesis is simple. Language has a tight generative grammar (morphemes) and a tight memory structure (events). Children learn language efficiently because their cognitive architecture exploits both. Modern LLMs do not exploit either explicitly, and they pay for it in parameters, compute, and data. The 2024–2026 wave of work on morpheme-aware tokenization, episodic memory for LLMs, and developmentally-plausible pretraining has produced every component MELM needs. What is missing is the synthesis and the corpus protocol.

We propose that synthesis. The MVP is a sub-1B model that reaches the conversational capability of a five-year-old, runs on CPU, and is built on a 100M-word corpus assembled via a reproducible four-stage pipeline. The four research questions in §7 gate scientific success; the milestones in §8 gate engineering progress. The roadmap is ~14 months.

This is, deliberately, not a frontier-model bid. It is a bet that the *structure* of language and memory has been under-exploited by the field, and that a small, opinionated model built on the right primitives is the most credible path to AI that is honest about how it understands.
